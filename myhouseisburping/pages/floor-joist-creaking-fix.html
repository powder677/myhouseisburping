Floor Joist Creaking: Why It Happens & How to Fix It

Floor joists creak because wood naturally shifts and flexes under weight, causing friction between the joist and its supports or fasteners. Most creaking is harmless seasonal movement, but persistent creaking can signal inadequate support, loose fasteners, or moisture damage. You can often fix it yourself by shimming gaps, upgrading nails to screws, or installing blocking—but serious structural issues require a professional engineer.

Why Your Floor Joists Creak: The Physics

Your floor joists are living wood. They absorb and release moisture with humidity changes, causing them to swell and shrink. When you or furniture puts weight on the floor above, the joist bends slightly. That movement creates friction where the wood rubs against support posts, ledgers, or where fasteners aren't holding properly. The result? A creak, squeak, or groan that travels through your house.

The problem gets worse if the joist was undersized for its span, if supports have settled, or if fasteners have loosened over decades. New homes often creak during the first year as wood acclimate to indoor humidity—this is normal. But if creaking worsens or appears suddenly in an older home, it's worth investigating.

Is It Structural Damage or Just Settling?

This is the question keeping you up at night. The good news: most creaking joists are not structural emergencies . However, you need to know the difference. Check our full guide on structural damage vs settling for a deeper analysis, but here's the quick version:

Sign Normal Settling Structural Problem
When it happens Predictable with temperature/humidity changes Random or worsening over time
Visible damage None; wood appears dry and solid Soft spots, rot, cracks, water stains, mold
Joist deflection Less than 1/2 inch visible sag over span Obvious sagging or more than 1 inch dip
Fasteners Tight when inspected Loose, missing, or rusted bolts/nails
Support bearing Full contact between joist and support Gaps or lifted edges showing daylight

If your joist shows rot, persistent sagging, or water damage, stop here and hire a structural engineer. The next steps are DIY fixes.

How to Locate the Creaking Joist

You can't fix what you can't find. Here's how to pinpoint the culprit:

  1. Get below the floor. Basement access is ideal; crawlspace works too. If you have neither, you'll need to work from above.
  2. Have someone walk above. While they walk slowly over the creaking area, listen and watch from below. The joist will flex visibly when weight hits it.
  3. Mark the spot. Use blue painter's tape to mark the joist and the exact location of the creak. This is your work zone.
  4. Check for obvious gaps. Look for daylight between the joist and its support beam or ledger. Gaps = movement = creaking.
  5. Inspect fasteners. Do you see loose nails that have backed out? Rusted bolts? Missing wedges? These are red flags.

If you don't have basement access, you can sometimes locate the joist from above by feeling vibration through the floor while someone walks. It's less precise but workable.

Common Causes of Floor Joist Creaking

Understanding the root cause determines which fix will work. For a broader look at causes of house noises , check our full article, but here are the joist-specific culprits:

  • Loose fasteners: Nails can back out as wood shrinks and the house settles. Lag bolts corrode or loosen. Screws don't seat fully. These allow micro-movement that creates noise.
  • Gaps between joist and support: Foundation settlement, beam deflection, or improper original installation can leave 1/4-inch gaps. The joist rocks slightly under load.
  • Missing or inadequate blocking: Blocking or bridging (cross-bracing between joists) prevents lateral flexing. If it's missing or deteriorated, adjacent joists rub together.
  • Moisture and wood movement: High humidity causes swelling; dry conditions cause shrinkage. This constant cycling loosens fasteners over time.
  • Rot or insect damage: Termites, carpenter ants, or fungal rot weaken the wood. The joist loses rigidity and moves more than it should.
  • Undersized joist: If the joist was too small for its span when installed, it's been deflecting (sagging) ever since. More deflection = more creaking.
  • Point loads above: A bathtub, water heater, or concentrated furniture load can over-stress a single joist, causing it to flex excessively.

DIY Fixes for Creaking Floor Joists

Once you've identified the joist and ruled out serious damage, try these fixes in order of difficulty and effectiveness:

1. Shim Gaps Between Joist and Support

This is the simplest fix and works for 60% of creak complaints. If you can see a gap between the joist bottom and the beam or ledger it rests on:

  1. Cut wooden shims (cedar shingles work great) to fit the gap.
  2. Drive shims from below with a hammer until they're snug. Don't over-tighten or you'll lift the joist.
  3. The joist should now have full bearing contact and won't rock under load.

Cost: $5–15 per joist. Time: 15 minutes per joist.

2. Upgrade Fasteners from Nails to Screws

If the joist is fastened with old nails that have backed out, replace them with 3-inch structural wood screws:

  1. Remove the old loose nails carefully with a pry bar.
  2. Drill pilot holes to prevent splitting.
  3. Drive structural screws (not deck screws—they're weaker) through the joist into the support below.
  4. Screws hold much better than nails and won't back out as the wood shrinks.

Cost: $2–4 per screw. Time: 30 minutes per joist.

3. Install Blocking or Bridging

If you notice that two adjacent joists seem to flex in opposite directions (one up while the other goes down), they're rocking against each other. Install solid blocking (short 2x pieces nailed horizontally between joists) every 4 feet along the span:

  1. Cut 2x material to fit snugly between joists.
  2. Drive 3-inch nails through the sides of adjacent joists into the blocking (4 nails total per block).
  3. This prevents lateral movement and couples the joists into a more rigid system.

Cost: $50–150 for materials. Time: 2–3 hours.

4. Install a Sister Joist

If a single joist is noticeably deflected or undersized, you can sister a new joist alongside it. This distributes the load between two members:

  1. Run a new joist parallel to the existing one, matching its size and depth.
  2. Bolt or screw them together every 12 inches with structural fasteners.
  3. Ensure both joists rest fully on the supports at each end.

Cost: $200–500 (material + fasteners). Time: 4–6 hours.

When to Call a Professional

You're beyond DIY territory if:

  • The joist shows soft spots, rot, or active termite damage.
  • The joist is visibly sagging (more than 1 inch over a 12-foot span).
  • Multiple joists are damaged or failing.
  • The house has foundation settlement or cracks in walls.
  • You're uncertain whether the joist is adequately supported.

A structural engineer can assess load capacity, recommend sistering or replacement, and ensure code compliance. Costs are $300–800 for an inspection.

Prevention: Stop Future Creaking

Once you've fixed the problem, prevent it from coming back:

  • Control humidity: Keep basements and crawlspaces between 30–60% RH with dehumidifiers or ventilation. This slows wood movement.
  • Fix water intrusion: Seal foundation cracks, insulate cold water pipes, and ensure gutters drain away from the house.
  • Check fasteners annually: Walk your basement or crawlspace once a year and look for backing-out nails or loose bolts.
  • Spread loads: Don't concentrate heavy furniture in one spot for years. Move it around occasionally.
  • Maintain support posts: Ensure columns are plumb and have adequate footings. Settling in one post can throw off joist alignment.

The Bottom Line
