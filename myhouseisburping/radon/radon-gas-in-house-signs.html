Radon Gas in Your House: Signs, Risks & How to Test

Radon has no smell, taste, or color—you cannot detect it without testing. The only real "sign" of radon is a test result above 4 pCi/L (picocuries per liter). You may notice increased respiratory problems among family members living in affected homes, but the only reliable way to know if your house has radon is to test for it using affordable kits or hiring a professional.

If you've heard about radon and started wondering whether your house is at risk, you're asking the right questions. Radon is a serious indoor air quality issue that affects millions of American homes, yet it remains invisible to our senses. This guide will help you understand what radon actually is, recognize the real (and false) signs of its presence, and show you exactly how to test for it without breaking the bank.

What Is Radon and Why Should You Care?

Radon is a radioactive gas that occurs naturally when uranium in soil and rock breaks down. It seeps into your home through cracks in foundations, gaps around pipes, and other openings in the basement or crawl space. Once inside, radon becomes trapped and accumulates to potentially dangerous levels.

The EPA identifies radon as the second leading cause of lung cancer in the United States, after smoking. It's responsible for approximately 21,000 deaths annually—more than drunk driving and house fires combined. The scary part? You could live in a high-radon home for years without feeling sick. Radon-induced lung cancer develops silently over time.

Your risk increases significantly if you smoke or have a family history of lung cancer. Even non-smokers face a real danger at high radon concentrations. This is why testing isn't optional—it's essential home maintenance, just like checking your water quality or inspecting your roof.

The Real Signs of Radon in Your House (And the Myths)

Here's what you absolutely need to know: radon produces no detectable signs through your senses. This is what makes it so dangerous. You cannot see it, smell it, taste it, or feel it. Your nose won't run. Your eyes won't water. You won't suddenly feel dizzy or cough.

However, there are indirect indicators you should understand:

Actual Indirect Signs

  • Persistent respiratory problems in family members —If multiple people in your household develop coughs, wheezing, or shortness of breath without an obvious cause (no new pets, no construction nearby), radon could be a factor. This is not a guaranteed sign, but it warrants testing.
  • Worsening asthma or allergy symptoms —Radon doesn't cause allergies, but it can irritate airways and trigger existing conditions. If your home's air quality seems poor despite cleaning, ventilating your home and testing for radon makes sense.
  • Basement or crawl space moisture problems —While moisture itself isn't a radon sign, the same entry points that let in moisture can let in radon. If your basement is damp, radon testing becomes more important.
  • Your geographic location —Radon varies dramatically by region. Some areas have consistently high radon levels, while others don't. Knowing your local radon risk helps you decide how urgently to test.

Common Myths About Radon Signs

Myth: "My house smells like radon." False. Radon is completely odorless. If your basement smells musty, that's mold or moisture—separate issues that should also be addressed. Either way, neither produces radon odor.

Myth: "If I felt fine, radon isn't present." False. Radon damage is cumulative and silent. You could feel perfectly healthy while radon steadily damages your lung tissue over years. This is exactly why testing is non-negotiable.

Myth: "I'll know if radon is a problem because I get sick." False. Radon-related lung cancer develops over decades without symptoms. You won't suddenly feel unwell from radon exposure the way you might from carbon monoxide (which causes headaches and dizziness).

Understanding Radon Levels: What Do the Numbers Mean?

The EPA has established 4 pCi/L (picocuries per liter) as the action level for radon. This is the concentration above which you should take steps to reduce radon in your home. Here's how to interpret your test results:

Radon Level (pCi/L) Risk Category Recommended Action
0–2 Very Low No action needed. Below EPA action level.
2–4 Low to Moderate Below action level, but consider fixing if 3–4 pCi/L. Retest in 2 years.
4–10 High Above action level. Plan radon mitigation within a few months.
10+ Very High Significantly above action level. Prioritize mitigation immediately.

Important note: The EPA recommends that any radon level below 4 pCi/L should be reduced if possible. Even at 2–4 pCi/L, you're still exposed to some risk, just at a lower level. Think of it like cholesterol—even "safe" levels are better when lower.

How to Test Your House for Radon: The Budget-Friendly Way

Testing is the only way to know your home's radon level. You have three main options:

1. DIY Short-Term Test (Most Affordable)

  • Cost: $10–$25 per kit
  • Duration: 2–7 days
  • How it works: You place a charcoal canister or electret detector in your basement or lowest occupied room, close windows and doors, and wait. Then mail it to the lab for analysis.
  • Best for: Quick screening and budget-conscious homeowners
  • Limitation: One test during one season isn't definitive. Radon levels fluctuate seasonally and even daily. For reliable results, experts recommend testing during winter (when homes are closed up and radon typically accumulates).

2. DIY Long-Term Test (More Reliable)

  • Cost: $20–$40 per kit
  • Duration: 3–12 months
  • How it works: Alpha track or electret detectors sit in your home for an extended period, averaging out fluctuations.
  • Best for: Getting an accurate picture of your home's actual radon exposure
  • Why it matters: A long-term test eliminates the guesswork from seasonal variations. If you're going to spend money fixing a radon problem, you want to know the real level first.

3. Professional Testing (Gold Standard)

  • Cost: $150–$300
  • Duration: Varies; usually 48 hours to 90 days
  • How it works: A certified radon professional uses calibrated equipment to measure levels and often provides a detailed report.
  • Best for: Confirming high results, preparing to sell your home, or when you're serious about mitigation
